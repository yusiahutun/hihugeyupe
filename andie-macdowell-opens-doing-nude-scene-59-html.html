Andie MacDowell opens up on doing first nude scene at 59

Publish date: 2024-10-09

Andie MacDowell said that doing her first nude scene at 59 in her new film Love After Love 'had no effect' on her.

The Jane by Design actress, speaking with The Hollywood Reporter on Sunday, said that playing her character, a woman named Suzanne who is grieving the death of her husband, had more of a resounding impact.

'What affected me more was to see how sad I looked,' the 59-year-old beauty said of her role in the film, which premiered this past weekend at the 2017 Tribeca Film Festival. 'The only reason I could do that is because I know that sadness.

'That to me made me feel more vulnerable than being naked. It had no effect on me, being naked, which is fascinating.'

The mother-of-three, who's been seen in films such as Sex, Lies, and Videotape, The Player and Short Cuts, said that she was uncomfortable going naked on film at the dawn of her acting career in the late 80s and early 90s.

'I'm not one to take my clothes off in a movie. Not that I'm a prude or anything, but I think I grew up in a time where most actresses would get body doubles,' MacDowell said. 'I was always very conscious of what my children thought. I never wanted to do anything to embarrass them.'

Change of pace: The actress said she previously avoided nude scenes as she 'grew up in a time where most actresses would get body doubles'

Connecting with her role: Andie said that she felt particularly vulnerable after watching herself in the independent drama 

The 5ft8 beauty said that she took a proactive approach after Love After Love director Russ Harbaugh informed her that her role in the film would include nude scenes.

He 'said to me, "How do you want to do this?" And I said, "Well, should I just show you?"' she said. 'And I went into the bathroom and started practicing what we're going to do.

'And I said, "What do you think?" And he said, "You're beautiful! You're just so beautiful! You're perfect!"'

Strong cast: Andie's co-stars in the film included Chris O'Dowd, James Adomian and Dree Hemingway 

In the interview, the Groundhog Day star, who celebrated her 59th birthday on Wednesday, said that she's kept mum about the challenge actresses face Hollywood as they grow older, but it remains a problem.

'When I turned 40, I could not have an interview without a journalist asking me, "What does it feel like to turn 40 and know that you're about not to be able to work?"' she said. 'There is a reality to it, but I never wanted to be one of those whiners. I feel like women come across as whiners when you constantly hear this.

She said that the show business game remains the same, nearly 20 years later.

'I'm 59, and nothing's changed since I was 40. Not much,' she said. 'I still don't want to be a whiner. But it is what it is.

'It is our reality, all women. It is the reality.'
