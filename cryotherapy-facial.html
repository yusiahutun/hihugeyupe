Are cryotherapy facial tools the key to brighter, firmer skin?

Publish date: 2024-04-19

Cryotherapy – the idea of subjecting your body to sub-zero temperatures (as low as -90 degrees) has transcended the trends to become a wellness mainstay, with freezing pods popping up all over the country.

Now, much like other alternative therapies – think gua sha and acupuncture – cryotherapy has been fine-tuned into facial treatments, designed to work specifically on our complexions.

But can we really freeze our faces into a brighter, tighter state of being? Here, we speak to the experts to ascertain whether cryotherapy facials, ice rollers and frozen skincare are worth the chill…

Related Story

How does a cryotherapy facial work?

“Whether cryotherapy is used on the body or face, it incites vasoconstriction – a reduction of the blood vessel size,” explains Dr. Yannis Alexandrides, cosmetic surgeon and founder of 111 Skin. This constriction can incite a vast array of benefits, including pain relief, muscle recovery and firmer skin.

According to Ada Ooi, facialist and founder of 001 Skincare, cryotherapy may be able to deliver a visible boost to the skin’s surface, from the inside out. “Cryotherapy uses sub-zero temperature to give a cold shock to the skin, which will send fresher blood to the targeted area,” she explains. “More blood means more oxygen, and a richer nutrient delivery, which flushes away toxins and reduces water retention.” The result? Skin that looks and feels plumped and refreshed – all within a matter of minutes.

The best cryotherapy facials

Several renowned facialists are now incorporating the principles of cryotherapy into their in-salon treatments.

The Spa de La Mer – La Mer’s first ever full-service treatment room in the UK, which opened earlier this year in Harrods – uses ice cubes in its signature London Detox facial to quickly cool and decrease puffiness. It’s an easy entry route into the concept of freezing facials.

Facialist Teresa Tarmey is another advocate for the benefits of chilling the skin: her signature treatments involve the use of her Cryo-Ball, which is made from stainless steel and whipped straight from a freezer. Similarly, Ada Ooi’s Cryo-Press – part of her own, excellent line – is worked into her entirely tailored treatments.

But it’s with 111Skin where things get technical. Available at Harvey Nichols, the brand’s unique Cryo Machine utilises a wand that blows -30 degree air onto the face. “This constricts the muscles, lifting and firming their appearance while calming redness, reducing pore size and sebum production,” explains Alexandrides. While it’s undoubtedly a less relaxing experience than a massage with a cold roller, the deeper penetration is said to offer more than surface-level benefits, tightening the skin on a lower level.

At-home cryo: ice rollers and frozen skincare

At-home options abound when it comes to cryo tools and treatments. As facialists seek to extend their powers beyond the salon, several have launched at-home products. Ooi, Tarmey and Georgia Louise have all created tools for use at home – and they’re a cinch to use – while Dr. Jart's clever rubber masks seal hydrating and calming ingredients into the skin.
