Oregon nurse accused of killing 65-year-old patient by switching his fentanyl drip for TAP WATER fac

Publish date: 2024-07-02

More than a dozen families have come forward to say their families were harmed at an Oregon hospital where a nurse is accused of swapping intravenous drugs for unsterilized tap water.

Police are investigating the Asante Rogue Regional Medical Center in Medford, after it reported suspicions that fentanyl intended for patients had been stolen by nurse Dani Marie Schofield.

The family of Horace Wilson is suing for $11.5 million after he died in from sepsis after bacteria flooded directly into his bloodstream from tap water in his iv drip.

Their lawyer Justin Idiart said he is now representing nine clients and speaking to another five whose loved ones died at the hospital between late 2021 and the middle of last year.

‘Their frustration is the lack of communication,’ he said, ‘They saw loved ones in the hospital for conditions that usually seem like they could have recovered from.’

According to the first lawsuit, a diluted IV drip reintroduced harmful bacterium into Wilson's blood, causing his condition to decline precipitously.

Only a month after first being admitted to Asante (pictured above), Horace Wilson died.

Lawyer Justin Idiart says more than a dozen families have come forward saying that their loved ones too were victims of the hospital 

Wilson was admitted in January 2022 after falling 10 feet from a ladder and damaging his spleen.

Doctors operated to remove it and his condition improved, but within days his condition took a 'turn for the worse,' after staphylococcus epidermidis - an infection-causing bacteria - entered his bloodstream, and 'became essentially impossible to eradicate’.

The hospital saw a five-fold increase in i/v blood infections between 2021 and 2022, according to federal data, and in November last year Schofield agreed to a voluntary nursing license suspension ‘pending the completion of an investigation’, according to the Oregon Board of Nursing.

In December the hospital told Medford police that they believed a former employee ‘was involved in the theft of fentanyl prescribed to patients resulting in some adverse patient outcomes’.

That month, the hospital ‘began contacting patients and their relatives telling them a nurse had replaced fentanyl with tap water causing bacterial infections'.

Fentanyl is one of the most commonly used as a painkiller in hospitals and comes with few direct side effects. 

But its powerful properties make it attractive to drug abusers and hospitals have recorded a growing number of thefts.

Two weeks after going into hospital, Wilson, a marijuana grower from Jacksonville, exhibited 'multiple clear markers of suffering from an infection,' suffering high temperatures and persistent hypotension, 'a hallmark of a state of sepsis’.

His state worsened, culminating in 'multi-system organ failure' and sepsis-induced brain dysfunction, and he died on February 25, after roughly a month in the hospital

Before he died, Wilson recovered enough cognitive function to tell the ICU staff that he 'no longer wished to live this way,' the lawsuit states.

The Medford Police Department said it had ‘received numerous calls from individuals asking if they or a family member have been impacted by the suspected actions of the former Asante employee’.

Officers have interviewed hundreds of people and examined thousands of documents, including medical records.

Dani Marie Schofield has not been charged with any crime and continues to maintain her innocence, telling The Lund Report that 'the truth will, I'm sure, come out.'

Before dying, Wilson told the ICU staff that he 'no longer wished to live this way.'

Both Schofield and Asante Rogue Regional Medical Center are listed as defendants in the first lawsuit.

Wilson's family is accusing the hospital of negligence, stating that the hospital either 'knew or should have known of the high likelihood of opioid diversion by one of its employees for their own use.'

‘We were distressed to learn of this issue,’ Asante said in a statement last month.

‘We reported it to law enforcement and are working closely with them.’
