KB Lager returns to bottle shops a decade after the legendary beer disappeared

Publish date: 2024-03-03

KB Lager - the famous brew remembered fondly as 'Kids' Beer', 'Cold Gold' and 'Rolled Gold' - is back in bottles shops a decade after it was discontinued. 

Cases of the distinctive gold cans have appeared in Liquorland and First Choice Liquor outlets in New South Wales as part of a limited release 'heritage run'.

Brewer CUB still owns the trademark to beers including KB which it no longer regularly makes due to changing tastes favouring craft brews. 

A trial batch of 5,000 cases of KB released in July has proved so popular CUB has produced 20,000 more, making 600,000 cans available for nostalgic drinkers.  

KB Lager - the famous brew remembered fondly as 'Kids' Beer', 'Cold Gold' and 'Rolled Gold' - is back in bottles shops a decade after it was discontinued

A pallet of KB arrived at First Choice Liquor in Maroubra in Sydney's eastern suburbs on October 26 and was given pride of place near the front door

KB was first brewed in Sydney at Tooth & Co's Kent Brewery - hence the name - in 1918 and was the dominant packaged beer in New South Wales for much of the 1970s and 1980s.

A series of television commercials during the golden age of Australian beer ads featured thirsty men with moustaches cracking open iced cans pulled from their Eskies. 

Park footballers were encouraged to emulate their professional counterparts after a hard game with the slogan 'Shake hands with a cold gold KB'.

The death of KB was announced in November 2011 after years of dwindling presence in bottle shops and pubs - and absolutely no promotion from CUB. 

An online petition calling for its return quickly appeared and Facebook pages were devoted to the drop, including Bring Back KB Lager.

'KB Lager is and always has been an Australian Icon,' an appeal on gopetition.com stated. 'So much has been lost over the years and this another great piece of Australiana has now been added to the list.

'Cracking a can of Cold Gold is every New South Welshman and woman's birth right.'

KB's greatest fan remains league boofhead Reg Reagan, who was regularly seen in his Footy Show appearances with a can resting on his 'Bring Back The Biff' T-shirt. Reagan, the creation of NRL star turned TV presenter Matt Jones, is pictured at the bar with a couple of cold KBs

A spokesman for CUB, which has Victoria Bitter, Fosters, Carlton Draught and Reschs in its portfolio, said the latest KB experiment had been hugely successful.  

'KB Lager is a classic NSW beer,' the spokesman said. 'For older beer lovers it brings back memories of simpler times in the 1980s and 90s while others remember it as the beer their dad used to drink.

'We know it still has its fans, particularly among older beer lovers, which is why we recently released it in a limited heritage run.

'But we've been blown away by how quickly its flown off the shelves recently. This is something we'll factor in as we plan our brewing runs for next year and beyond.'

KB, which is selling for $54 a carton, once came in dimpled steel cans but is now found in the standard aluminium. 

It was unclear if the latest batch was brewed to the original recipe but Liquorland described it as having 'subtle, fruity flavours, slightly malty sweetness and light body.'

Liquorland patrons have welcomed back KB to the bottle shop's shelves with five-star reviews

Some reviewers remembered a sweeter flavour. 'Very good drop but not quite as sweet as I recall back in the eighties,' one wrote. 'Loved it anyway.'

'Never tried it before but my father and father-in-law told me many times how much they loved it when younger and after downing one I can understand why,' a new convert stated.

KB's famous dimpled steel cans have been replaced by aluminium containers 

A third said: 'Remember drinking this underage and we joked it stood for "Kids' Beer" only to find it wasn't on tap anywhere when I was legal.'

For a long time the KB logo was more often seen on classic old advertising posters outside pubs than inside hotel fridges.

It experienced a brief resurgence early this century when the cold gold can was adopted by drinkers at inner-city bars and CUB was making just 16,000 cases a year.

'Everyone starts going on about "the old man" and fetching it from the fridge for him,' one Oxford Street bar manager said in 2004. 

'It's very nostalgic. We get that a lot - steel can, cold gold, Sydney Cricket Ground on the Hill.'

A cold KB also remained the drink of choice while it lasted for football fans attending Newtown Jets home games at Henson Park in Marrickville.

KB's greatest fan was league boofhead Reg Reagan, who was regularly seen in his Footy Show appearances with a can resting on his 'Bring Back The Biff' T-shirt.

A cold KB also remained the drink of choice while it lasted for football fans attending Newtown Jets home games at Henson Park in Marrickville (pictured)

Reagan, the creation of NRL star turned television presenter Matthew Johns, explained in 2004 that latter-day KB drinkers were trying to recapture something of their childhoods.

'My old man, Ray Reagan, he used to scull his KB,' said Reagan, who celebrated his First Communion and many other milestones with Kids' Beer. 

Reagan recalled sneaking up on his dad while Ray watched the long-running police drama series Cop Shop in the family's Cessnock lounge room.

'I'd just walk up and I'd have a little sip out of the KB,' Reagan reminisced. 'I think we all relate to those days.' 

Two KB cans were placed on display during a memorial service for the late Wests and Newtown rugby league icon Tommy Raudonikis at the SCG following his death in April.
